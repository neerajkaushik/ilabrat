Having heard learned counsel on either side , we have carefully gone through the material on record . Apparently , there is no eyewitness to the incident and the case is entirely based upon circumstantial evidence . In such a case , the Court is expected to be more careful while analyzing the evidence and convicting the accused . In other words , in all probabilities , the chain of circumstances should lead to the irresistible conclusion that the accused participated in the commission of crime and committed the offence . This Court has long back set the mode of evaluating circumstantial evidence in Hanumant Govind Nargundkar Vs . State of Madhya Pradesh , 1953 CriLJ 129 in the following terms : " It is well to remember that in cases where the evidence is of a circumstantial nature , the circumstances from which the conclusion of guilt is to be drawn should in the first instance be fully established , and all the facts so established should be consistent only with the hypothesis of the guilt of the accused .
